## Input

Who you are: Founder of a workflow software company for finance teams. Spent 7 years as a CFO before starting this company.

What you keep noticing: Most finance software is sold on dashboards, but finance leaders actually need fewer surprises. A lot of AI for back office work is being marketed like magic when the real value is operational confidence.

What you've lived through: I had to explain missed numbers to a board twice because our reporting process was fragmented and late. That shaped how I think about software promises.

Things you do not want to sound like: generic SaaS founder, AI maximalist

## Output

Core thesis:
The best finance software is not the tool with the prettiest dashboard - it's the one that reduces surprise for the person accountable for the numbers.

Three content pillars:

1. Operational confidence
Own the idea that finance teams buy certainty, not features. This comes directly from having had to defend numbers in real board settings.
Example post angle: "Most finance tools market visibility. Finance leaders are actually buying fewer bad surprises."

2. Anti-magic AI
Argue that AI in the back office should be judged by traceability and reliability, not by how futuristic the demo feels.
Example post angle: "If your AI workflow saves time but creates one unexplained variance, the CFO will never trust it again."

3. Accountability from experience
Use lived operating pain as the source of authority. The POV lands because it is grounded in consequences, not opinion for its own sake.
Example post angle: "People who have never had to explain a miss to the board build very different software."

Five post angles:
- Why "visibility" is an overrated promise in finance software
- The hidden tax of fragmented reporting workflows
- What finance leaders actually mean when they ask for automation
- Why trust beats novelty in AI products sold to back-office teams
- The product decisions you make differently after you've had to defend a forecast

The underrated take:
Finance leaders do not want more insight if it arrives without accountability. They want systems that make accountability easier to carry.

What to avoid:
- Generic "future of AI" commentary - it weakens the specificity that makes this POV credible.
- Advice framed like universal startup wisdom - your edge is operator realism, not founder inspiration.
